Iran war spikes oil prices globally while AI targets military decisions and Chrome zero-days land.

Morning Brief — 2026-03-13

The dominant signal today is geopolitical and economic: a US-Israel war with Iran is choking oil shipments through the Strait of Hormuz, sending the Korean won back toward 1,500, driving Malaysia’s fuel subsidy costs up fourfold, and prompting ships to broadcast Chinese affiliations to avoid attack. That’s not background noise — it’s a supply chain event with real downstream effects on energy costs, logistics, and inflation. On the tech side, two stories demand attention: Google pushed an emergency Chrome patch for two zero-days already under active exploitation, and multiple outlets are reporting that the Pentagon is actively demoing AI chatbots — including Anthropic’s Claude — for targeting and war-plan generation, a signal that defense AI procurement is accelerating fast. The Iran situation is confirmed across multiple independent sources but flag all geopolitical details for Geopolitical Caution. That’s the brief. Coffee’s getting cold.

Tier 1 — Act or Prepare Now

Google Pushes Emergency Chrome Patch for Two Actively Exploited Zero-Days

Confidence: low | Sources: The Register | Category: networking

Google released an emergency Chrome update fixing two previously unknown vulnerabilities — one in the Skia graphics library and one in the V8 JavaScript engine — that were already being exploited in the wild before patches shipped. This brings Chrome’s actively exploited bug count to three in 2026.

Why it matters: Any organization running Chrome browsers — which is most of them — is exposed until patched. Unpatched zero-days in V8 are high-severity; attackers have active exploit code in circulation right now.

Action: Push Chrome updates to all endpoints today. Verify enterprise fleet patch status before end of business.

Source: The Register

Iran War Driving Oil Spike — Korean Markets Down 3%, Won Hits 1,500

Confidence: low | Sources: Hankyung | Category: finance/semiconductors

KOSPI fell roughly 3% in early trading as oil prices surged, with the Korean won touching 1,500 to the dollar again. The moves are attributed to escalating Middle East conflict — a US-Israel war with Iran — disrupting global oil and gas shipments.

Why it matters: Semiconductor and electronics supply chains run through South Korea. A weak won raises input costs for Samsung and SK Hynix. Sustained oil price spikes translate into higher energy and logistics costs across the chip and networking infrastructure supply chain.

Action: Monitor KRW/USD and oil benchmarks through the session; flag procurement or contract pricing conversations that assumed stable energy costs.

Source: SEC EDGAR Latest Filings

Tier 2 — Monitor

Pentagon Demos AI Chatbots for Targeting Decisions — Claude Named

Confidence: low | Sources: Wired, MIT Technology Review | Category: tech/AI

A Defense Department official revealed the US military is exploring use of generative AI to rank targets and recommend strike order. Palantir demos and Pentagon records show Anthropic’s Claude among systems being evaluated. Bloomberg’s Project Maven coverage adds context, describing how Silicon Valley AI is now being tested in the Iran conflict.

Why it matters: This signals accelerating defense AI procurement — a major demand vector for AI infrastructure, compute, and networking. It also creates regulatory and reputational risk for AI vendors with enterprise customers who may have mixed feelings about military use cases.

Revisit: Next week — watch for Congressional response or Anthropic public statement on military use policy.

Source: MIT Technology Review

Strait of Hormuz Partially Blocked — Ships Spoofing Chinese Affiliations

Confidence: low | Sources: South China Morning Post | Category: china/geopolitics

At least 11 vessels in the Gulf of Oman and Persian Gulf are broadcasting Chinese ownership signals via AIS transponders to avoid Iranian attack, per Kpler/MarineTraffic data. Iran’s new supreme leader has vowed to keep the Hormuz strait closed.

Why it matters: Hormuz handles roughly 20% of global oil transit. Disruption directly affects energy prices for data center operators, logistics costs for chip shipments, and broader inflation. AIS spoofing indicates the blockade is being taken seriously by commercial shippers.

Action: If your organization has supply chain exposure to Gulf shipping lanes (components, energy contracts), flag for procurement review.

Revisit: Daily — situation is fluid.

Source: South China Morning Post

Apple Cuts China App Store Commission to 25% Under Beijing Pressure

Confidence: low | Sources: The Register, South China Morning Post | Category: china/tech

Why it matters: Apple making fee concessions under political pressure in China is a signal of continued regulatory leverage Beijing holds over foreign tech platforms — relevant context for any company weighing China market exposure or App Store-dependent revenue.

Revisit: 2 weeks — watch for similar pressure on other platform fees or follow-on regulatory moves.
